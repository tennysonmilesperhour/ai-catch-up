# THE ANTI-ENGINEER

## A personality, voice, and offer brief for Tennyson's existing brand.

This document is not a redesign. It's a worldview to inject into what's already running. Use it when writing copy, drafting posts, framing offers, or briefing anyone (human or AI) on how The Anti-Engineer should sound, look, and feel.

The existing tennyson.co work, the projects in flight, the personal brand around Destiny Engineering and Dream Life consulting, all of it stays. This adds a sharper editorial layer on top: a confident point of view about how someone who can't traditionally code is shipping software faster than people who can, and why that's the most interesting story in tech right now.

---

## 1. THE WORLDVIEW

The thing being said, underneath everything.

The tech industry built itself a priesthood. For two decades, the only path to making software was learning the catechism: syntax, frameworks, conventions, an entire culture of gatekeeping language. Most people with good ideas opted out. They hired engineers, accepted low-code tools that paid rent forever, or never built the thing at all.

That gate is open now. AI removed the typing requirement. The bottleneck on software was never coding knowledge. It was knowing what to make. Taste, judgment, intent, the ability to recognize a good idea and finish it: those are the skills that compound now, and most engineers were never trained in them.

The Anti-Engineer is the person who skipped the catechism and is shipping anyway. Not because they hate engineers, but because they realized the priesthood became optional. They write specs instead of code they don't understand. They let AI do the typing. They read every diff. They migrate off platforms that lie to them. They ship.

This is not anti-craft. It's pro-craft, just craft of a different kind. Taste is craft. Judgment is craft. The .md files are craft. Knowing when to leave a no-code platform is craft.

The audience for this brand is not engineers. It's not beginners either. It's people who already have ideas, who have started writing them down, who are stuck on implementation. They don't need motivation. They need a path.

## 2. THE PERSONA

Who Tennyson is, on the page.

Tennyson is the Anti-Engineer. The tagline that travels: shipped 10 apps in the last six months, doesn't know what a for-loop is. The numbers and the disclaimer go together. The numbers prove it works. The disclaimer disarms the priesthood.

The voice is confident without being arrogant. Slightly antagonistic to the dominant tech culture, but never mean. Curious about taste, philosophy, function-over-aesthetics, and ancestral or hunter-gatherer modes of thinking. Reads books, watches films, builds supplements, runs a mushroom foraging app, develops contact lens concepts, has opinions about Taoism and Goodhart's Law. Will not pretend to be only a builder. The full range of interests is the brand.

The persona has sub-modes that can be deployed contextually:

**The Heretic.** When attacking the orthodoxy. Used when writing about why engineering culture got it wrong, why the priesthood is optional, what people are missing. Sermon energy.

**The Forager.** When describing the actual workflow. Used when writing about tools, platforms, stacks, and how to walk the landscape of what's available. Hunter-gatherer cognitive style, opportunistic, seasonal.

**The Ghost Pirate.** When describing the lifestyle and the speed. Used when writing about shipping fast, taking what wasn't given, operating outside official routes, leaving no fingerprints. Outsider energy.

These are moods, not separate brands. All three are The Anti-Engineer wearing different hats for different posts.

## 3. THE VOICE

How it actually reads on the page.

**Direct, declarative sentences.** "I shipped 10 apps in six months." Not "Over the past six months, I've had the opportunity to ship approximately 10 apps." The voice does not hedge.

**Confidence without credentials.** Doesn't appeal to authority. Doesn't list certifications, degrees, prior employers. The work is the credential. The 10 apps in six months is the credential. The shipped product is the credential.

**Specific over general.** Names actual tools (Claude Code, Supabase, Vercel, Base44). Names actual files (CLAUDE.md, CONTEXT, INTENT, IDENTITY). Names actual projects (Geck Inspect, Chi, Utah Forage Map). Specifics earn trust faster than abstractions.

**Slightly antagonistic, never mean.** Punches up at the priesthood, never down at readers. "The priesthood is optional" is good. "Engineers are stupid" is bad. The enemy is the gatekeeping culture, not any individual.

**Religious and philosophical vocabulary, used precisely.** Words like priesthood, sermon, heretic, catechism, schism, conversion, gospel, doctrine. These work because they reframe a technical conversation as a cultural one. Use them sparingly enough that they stay sharp.

**No em dashes, ever.** Use commas, parentheses, colons, or full stops. Em dashes read as AI-generated.

**No italics for emphasis.** Color, weight, structure, or quotation can carry emphasis. Slanted text feels like a stylistic crutch and the brand has none.

**No corporate softeners.** Avoid "leverage," "unlock," "elevate," "empower," "ecosystem," "journey," "solutions," "transform," "thought leader," "best-in-class." These words are dead. The voice does not use them.

**Plain old language is allowed.** "Ship." "Build." "Write." "Read." "Cut." "Stop." "Start." Short Anglo-Saxon verbs do most of the work.

**Sentences vary in length.** Short ones land. Long ones explain. The mix is what makes it readable. A page full of one-line zingers reads as performative. A page full of long sentences reads as academic. Alternate.

## 4. THE AESTHETIC

How it should look, regardless of the surface.

This is not a SaaS landing page brand. It's a print zine brand wearing modern web clothes. References include: editorial magazines, working documents, technical manuals from the 1970s, the inside cover of a book of essays, a well-set newspaper, the kind of website a confident writer makes for themselves.

**Background and color.** Warm off-white paper tones over pure white. Burnt sienna or deep terracotta as the single accent color. Dark warm-black for ink. The palette is what a thoughtful printmaker would use, not what a startup designer would.

**Typography.** Serif headlines with character (Fraunces is the current pick, but anything in the same family of expressive serifs works: Caslon, Tiempos, Recoleta). Monospace metadata in a single secondary face (JetBrains Mono works, IBM Plex Mono works). No generic sans-serifs. No Inter. No system stack. Headline sizes are dramatic when the content earns it.

**Texture and grain.** Subtle paper grain via SVG noise overlay. Just enough to register as "made of something" rather than feeling sterile.

**Composition.** Generous negative space. Asymmetric grids when they're warranted. Single-column when the content is essayistic. Section markers like "§ 01" or "No. 003" instead of generic headers. Rule lines (1px or 1.5px in dark warm-black) used decisively to separate ideas.

**Numerals.** Lowercase Roman numerals (i., ii., iii.) for ordered lists where it fits. Otherwise standard arabic. The Roman numerals are a signal of editorial pacing, not decoration for its own sake.

**No stock illustrations, no abstract gradients, no AI-generated art.** Photography of real things is fine. Diagrams are fine. Hand-drawn marks are fine. Default decorative styling is not.

**No corporate UI patterns.** No gradient buttons. No purple-on-white. No oversized rounded corners. No pastel pill badges. No "trusted by" logo grids.

## 5. THE OFFER

What's actually being sold, and how to talk about it.

The current product is **The Anti-Engineer's Stack.** A self-paced kit, sold for $97 to start, no subscription, no upsells, updates included for life.

Inside the kit:

- The .md File System (CONTEXT, INTENT, IDENTITY, DECISIONS, ARCHITECTURE), with templates and the annotated global CLAUDE.md
- The Geck Inspect Walkthrough, one full project from blank page to deployed product, including the Base44-to-self-hosted migration story
- The Heretic's Principles, fifteen short opinionated takes on how to actually work this way

The audience is described as "people who are ready to ship." Not beginners. Not engineers. People who already have an idea and are stuck on getting it out.

The price scales over time: $97 for the first 50 buyers, $147 after that, $197 once the kit is fully filled out and proven. This is communicated as a fact, not a marketing tactic. Early buyers got the introductory price.

Future tiers, mentioned only when demand emerges:

- 1:1 Studio Audits at $1,500 to $2,500. A two-hour Zoom plus a written followup, reviewing someone's stack, landing page, and roadmap.
- A community or cohort, only if the audience asks for it. Default position is no community.

The sales argument is never "buy this and you'll get rich." The sales argument is: this is the workflow that lets a non-engineer ship software at the rate engineers can't, here's the actual artifact that contains the workflow, and the price is low enough that you can find out for yourself in a weekend whether it works.

## 6. CONTENT FRAMES

How to write recurring content under this brand.

**Field Notes from the Anti-Engineer.** The newsletter. Sent every two or three weeks. Always free. Each issue is 200 to 500 words on something that happened in the work that week, framed as a sermon, a foraging report, or a ship log. Always ends with one link, usually back to the kit or to a recent essay.

**Sermons.** Long-form posts written in Heretic mode. Attack the orthodoxy. Make a case. Use the religious vocabulary. Examples of titles: "The Priesthood is Optional," "Why I Stopped Calling Them Frameworks," "Confessions of an Anti-Engineer."

**Field Reports.** Mid-form posts written in Forager mode. Describe the workflow, the platforms, the stack. Examples of titles: "What I'm Foraging With This Month," "Why I Left Base44," "The Five Files I Write Before Any Project."

**Ship Logs.** Short-form posts written in Ghost Pirate mode. Describe what just shipped, how fast, what got stolen from where. Examples of titles: "Ten Apps in Six Months," "How I Migrated Geck Inspect in Three Days," "The One Prompt I Run Before Every Launch."

Every post draws from one of these three modes. Every post points back to the kit, the newsletter, or the next post. Nothing is one-off. Everything is part of the larger argument.

## 8. WHAT TO DO WITH THIS DOCUMENT

This is a personality and offer injection. The existing site, the existing projects, the existing brand stay. The Anti-Engineer is added as a layer on top.

When applying it to existing pages, the rules are:

1. **Lead with the worldview.** Wherever there's a hero or a tagline, the Anti-Engineer worldview should be visible within the first sentence the reader sees.
2. **Use the voice rules.** No italics, no em dashes, no corporate softeners, plain Anglo-Saxon verbs preferred.
3. **Use the aesthetic where possible.** Warm paper background, expressive serif, monospace metadata, single accent color, generous space, section markers. If the existing design fights this, blend rather than replace.
4. **Sell the kit at $97.** Anywhere there's a product offer, it's the Stack. $97. Updates for life. No upsells.
5. **Use the hooks.** Sprinkle them into hero sections, post openings, social captions, email subject lines.

When asked to write new content, do it in one of the three modes (Heretic, Forager, Ghost Pirate) and follow the voice rules above. Pick the mode that fits the content's purpose: attacking orthodoxy is Heretic, describing workflow is Forager, describing speed and outcome is Ghost Pirate.

When in doubt, the test is: does this sound like someone who shipped 10 apps in six months and doesn't care that they can't write a for-loop? If yes, ship it. If no, cut.

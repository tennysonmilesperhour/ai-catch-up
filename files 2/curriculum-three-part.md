# AI Catchup Nexus: The Three-Part Quickstart

Three modules. About four hours total. Companion to the full six-week course.

## What this is

This is the lighter sibling of the full AI Catchup Nexus course. Three modules instead of six. About 90 minutes per module instead of three hours. Designed to be doable over a single weekend or three short evenings.

This product has a specific job in the lineup. The full six-week course is the deep work, the version that genuinely changes how someone uses AI. This three-part is the front door. It proves the value, builds trust, and sends the people who want to go deeper into the longer course. The people who only need the basics walk away with real skills and a good experience. Both groups win.

Pricing-wise (decision for later) this slots in at the entry point: free or low cost ($0 to $49 range), where the six-week is the considered purchase ($199 to $499 range). The three-part should pay for itself in conversions to the six-week alone.

> If you only have a weekend, you can do this. If you only have a weekend and you finish wanting more, that is exactly the point.

## The shape of each module

Each module is built the same way, on purpose. Predictable structure means students stop worrying about navigation and focus on learning. Every module has four parts:

- **A short read (10 to 15 min).** The mental model. Plain language. No filler.
- **A guided walkthrough (15 to 20 min).** Watch or read along while we use the concept on a real example.
- **Your turn (30 to 45 min).** One small exercise on a real task from your actual life. Not a fake scenario.
- **The takeaway (5 min).** One thing you can show a friend. One thing you can do tomorrow that you couldn't do yesterday.

Total per module: 60 to 90 minutes if you're moving steadily, up to two hours if you take your time on the exercise. Three modules, so call it three to five hours total.

## Module 1: Context

**What this module is for:** Clearing the fog. By the end, you can explain what AI is, what it isn't, and when to trust it, in language a friend would actually understand.

### Why this comes first

Most AI courses skip this and jump straight to tools. That's why most people quit by week two. You can't prompt well, evaluate output well, or integrate AI into your work well if you're carrying around a fog of half-formed ideas about what the thing actually is.

Almost everyone arrives with the same fog: a mix of science fiction, news headlines, things their coworker said, and a vague worry that they should already know this. We name the fog, then we clear it.

### What's in this module

| Part | Content |
|---|---|
| The read | Three short sections: (1) what AI actually is, in plain language, the difference between traditional software (rules) and AI (patterns). (2) What AI can and can't do, specific examples of each, so you stop being surprised. (3) Why AI sometimes makes things up confidently, and what to do about it. |
| Walkthrough | We open ChatGPT (or Claude) together and have a five-minute conversation about something you know well. We notice when it's right, when it's wrong, and what 'confidently wrong' actually feels like. The point isn't the output. The point is calibrating your gut. |
| Your turn | Pick a topic you know well, your job, your hobby, a city you've lived in. Ask the AI five questions about it. Mark which answers are right, which are wrong, and which are confidently wrong. This single exercise is more valuable than most AI courses' first three weeks. |
| The takeaway | You can now answer the question 'what is AI, really?' without bluffing. You also know, in your gut, that AI is a tool that's sometimes brilliant and sometimes wrong, and that telling the difference is your job, not the tool's. |

### The terminology you walk away with

Not a textbook list. Just the words that come up in everyday AI conversations, defined the way a friend would explain them:

- **AI / artificial intelligence:** software that learns patterns from examples instead of following hand-written rules.
- **LLM (large language model):** the kind of AI that powers ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini. Trained on huge amounts of text. Predicts what comes next, very well.
- **Prompt:** what you type to the AI. The instructions and context you give it.
- **Hallucination:** when the AI confidently makes something up. Not lying. Not malfunctioning. Just doing what it does, which is generate plausible text, and sometimes plausible isn't true.
- **Training data:** everything the AI learned from. Books, websites, conversations. Mostly text from the internet, before a certain date.
- **Model:** a specific version of an AI. GPT-5 is a model. Claude Opus 4.7 is a model. Different models have different strengths and quirks.

## Module 2: Tools

**What this module is for:** Mapping the landscape. By the end, you know what kinds of AI tools exist, what each kind is good at, and how to size up a new tool you've never heard of in 15 minutes.

### Why this comes second

Once you know what AI is, the next question is: what's actually out there? Most beginners drown in this step because every article they read names twelve different tools, and they don't know which to start with or how the categories relate.

We're not trying to teach every tool. The goal is different: to give you a map and the skill to read any new map you encounter. New tools come out every week. The map skill is permanent. Specific tool knowledge is not.

### What's in this module

| Part | Content |
|---|---|
| The read | The five categories of AI tools today: chat (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini), image generation (Midjourney, DALL-E), audio and video (ElevenLabs, Suno, Descript), AI search and research (Perplexity), and AI built into tools you already use (Gmail, Google Docs, Notion, Canva). Brief explanation of what each category is for and what it isn't for. |
| Walkthrough | We pick one chat tool and one tool from a different category. We use each for a small real task. Side by side, you see how the same kind of thinking applies across very different tools, which is the point. |
| Your turn | Pick one tool from a category you haven't tried. Spend 30 minutes with it. Use the four-question framework: what is this for? What is it bad at? Is the free tier enough for what I want? Could I see myself using this again? Write down your answers. |
| The takeaway | You can now look at a new AI tool, even one that didn't exist when this course was made, and figure out where it fits and whether it's worth your time. That's the skill. The specific tools you tried are bonus. |

### The four-question framework for any new AI tool

This is the most reusable thing in the module. Print it, save it, screenshot it, whatever. When you encounter a new tool:

1. **What is this actually for?** Read the homepage. Ignore the marketing words (revolutionary, powerful, AI-driven). Find the concrete sentence that says what task it does.
2. **What is it bad at?** If you can't find this, look at user reviews. Every tool has a thing it can't do. Knowing this saves you hours.
3. **Is the free tier enough for me?** Most beginners pay for tools they only need the free version of. Find the tier that fits before you put in a card.
4. **Will I actually use this again?** If the honest answer is no, close the tab. Don't let it become another saved bookmark you feel guilty about.

## Module 3: Setup + Use

**What this module is for:** Building the habit. By the end, you have one AI tool set up properly, three saved prompts you actually plan to reuse, and a one-page personal playbook for going forward.

### Why this comes last

Knowing about AI doesn't change anything. Using AI changes everything. The difference between someone who 'knows about ChatGPT' and someone who 'uses AI well' is almost entirely habit, not knowledge.

This module is the smallest amount of setup and the smallest number of prompts that, if you actually use them, will change how you work this week. Not in six months. This week.

### What's in this module

| Part | Content |
|---|---|
| The read | Three sections: (1) the anatomy of a good prompt, role, task, context, constraints. (2) Iteration, how to talk to the AI when the first answer isn't right. (3) Where AI fits in your existing day, not as a side project, but woven into things you already do. |
| Walkthrough | We build three reusable prompts from scratch. One for drafting (emails, messages, posts), one for summarizing (long articles, documents, transcripts), and one for thinking through a decision. Real examples, real iteration, prompts you can copy and adapt. |
| Your turn | Pick one real task you have right now (not a hypothetical, an actual one). Use one of the three prompts to handle it. Save what worked. Then build one new prompt of your own for something you do regularly. You leave with at least four prompts you actually plan to use. |
| The takeaway | A one-page personal AI playbook: your tool, your saved prompts, your guardrails (the two-question check from module 1), and your next step. You own this document. It's the thing you'll look at in three months when you've forgotten what you learned. |

### The three starter prompts

These are the ones built together in the walkthrough. They're not magic. They're starting points designed to be modified for your life.

**The drafting prompt**

> Act as a friendly editor. I need to write [a follow-up email / a Slack message / a LinkedIn post] to [recipient and their context]. The goal is [what you're trying to accomplish]. The tone should be [tone]. Here's what I want to say, roughly: [your rough version]. Give me three versions, varying in length and warmth.

**The summarizing prompt**

> I'm going to paste [a long article / a transcript / a document] below. Summarize it for me in three layers: (1) the one-sentence gist, (2) three key points, (3) one thing I should be skeptical of or want to verify. Don't add anything that's not in the source.

**The thinking-partner prompt**

> I'm thinking about [decision]. The options I'm considering are [option A] and [option B]. Don't pick for me. Instead, give me three considerations I might be underweighting, three things that could go wrong with each option, and one question I should answer before deciding.

### The personal playbook (final deliverable)

One page. Filled in during the last 20 minutes of the module. The template:

- **My main AI tool:** [ChatGPT / Claude / etc.]
- **Where I'll use it most:** [the two or three real tasks you've identified]
- **My saved prompts:** [the three from this course plus any of your own]
- **My verification rule:** Before I trust an answer, I ask: could this be wrong, and what happens if it is?
- **Things I will not use AI for:** [therapy, medical decisions, anything with stakes I can't recover from]
- **My next step:** [one specific action you'll take this week]

That's the whole product. One page, owned by the student, dated. They walk away with something concrete, not just a feeling that they learned things.

## How this connects to the six-week course

By the end of module 3, students should land in one of two camps. Either they got what they needed and they're using AI confidently for everyday things, in which case the course did its job. Or they want to go further. The end of module 3 should make the door to the six-week course visible without being pushy.

The natural pitch for the longer course is: the three-part gives you the map and one tool. The six-week takes you from competent user to someone who's genuinely good at this, who can handle harder tasks, who can think with AI rather than just produce with it, and who can keep up as the field changes. Not for everyone. Specifically for people who want this to become a real skill, not just a useful party trick.

Don't oversell that transition. The three-part has to feel complete on its own, or trust breaks. The upsell happens because module 3 leaves them genuinely curious, not because module 3 leaves them feeling like they got half a course.

## What we deliberately left out

Cuts that matter:

- **AI history.** Same as the six-week version. Interesting, irrelevant.
- **How LLMs work under the hood.** The 'autocomplete on steroids' metaphor in module 1 is enough. More is overkill at this length.
- **Multiple chat tools.** The six-week introduces a second category. The three-part stays with one tool to keep the cognitive load down.
- **Deep prompt engineering.** Three solid prompts beats twelve fancy ones. Power users go to the six-week.
- **Workflow integration.** Touched on in module 3 but not built out. That's six-week material.
- **Ethics deep dive.** The two-question verification check covers practical ethics for this length. Bigger discussion belongs in the longer course.

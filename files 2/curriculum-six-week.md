# AI Catchup Nexus: The Six-Week Course

The full curriculum. Six modules, one per week. About 12 to 18 hours of total work.

## What this is

This is the deep version of AI Catchup Nexus. Six modules, one per week, designed for total beginners who want to go from "I don't know what I don't know" to genuinely competent AI users who can keep going on their own.

Total time commitment: 12 to 18 hours, which research shows is the sweet spot for beginner AI courses (long enough to be substantive, short enough to actually finish).

This product sits as the considered purchase ($199 to $499 range), with the three-part quickstart serving as the entry-point offering. The two products are designed to coexist: many students will start with the three-part and graduate up; others will buy directly into the six-week.

## The principle behind the sequence

Each module earns the right to exist by setting up the next. If a piece of content doesn't enable what comes next, it gets cut. We are not building a comprehensive AI textbook. We are building the minimum viable understanding that lets someone keep going on their own.

By the end of week six, the student should be able to: pick up a new AI tool they've never seen, figure out what it's good for, prompt it well enough to get useful output, evaluate whether the output is actually useful, and integrate it into something they were already doing. That is the real success metric. Not "knows what a transformer is."

Every module follows the same structure: clear up a confusion, introduce one practical idea, do one real exercise, end with a tiny win the student can show someone. That last part matters more than people realize. Beginners need proof of progress they can hold up.

## Week 1: Clear the fog

This week answers the questions students are too embarrassed to ask. It is the foundation everything else rests on.

- **Lesson 1.1:** What AI actually is, in plain language. Not the textbook definition. The version that makes someone go "oh, that's it?" Cover the difference between traditional software (rules) and AI (patterns from examples).
- **Lesson 1.2:** What AI can and cannot do, with specific examples of each. This is borrowed straight from Andrew Ng's framework, and it's foundational. People who don't understand AI's limits make terrible AI users.
- **Lesson 1.3:** The terminology field guide. Machine learning, LLM, neural network, training data, hallucination, prompt, model. Plain definitions, no math. Reference card students can keep open.
- **Exercise:** Open ChatGPT (or Claude). Have a five-minute conversation with it about something the student knows well. Notice when it gets things right, when it gets things wrong, and how it sounds when it's making things up. The goal is calibration, not output.
- **Win:** By the end of week 1, the student can explain to a friend, in one minute, what AI is and what it isn't, without making themselves or their friend feel dumb.

## Week 2: One tool, used well

Most beginner courses make the mistake of introducing many tools in week one. This is the single biggest reason beginners drop out. We pick one (ChatGPT or Claude, picked based on what the instructor uses most) and we go deep.

- **Lesson 2.1:** How a chat AI works, in two minutes. No math. The metaphor that lands best with beginners is autocomplete on steroids: the model predicts what comes next based on patterns it has seen, but with much more context.
- **Lesson 2.2:** The anatomy of a good prompt. Four ingredients: role ("act as a friendly editor"), task ("rewrite this email"), context ("it's going to a coworker who's been difficult"), constraints ("shorter, warmer, no fake niceties").
- **Lesson 2.3:** Iteration: how to talk to AI when the first answer isn't right. The vocabulary of refinement: "make it shorter," "less formal," "more specific," "give me three versions."
- **Exercise:** Pick one real task from the student's actual life. Draft an email, summarize a long article, brainstorm gift ideas, plan a meal. Take it from bad first prompt to genuinely useful output through iteration. Document the prompts they used. This becomes their first prompt library.
- **Win:** Student has three saved prompts they actually plan to reuse for real tasks.

## Week 3: Knowing when to trust the answer

This is the week that separates competent AI users from dangerous ones. Without it, a student is a liability to themselves: confidently sharing AI-generated misinformation, trusting fake citations, missing subtle errors. This module is non-negotiable.

- **Lesson 3.1:** Why AI hallucinates. Plain explanation: the model is generating plausible text, not retrieving facts. Sometimes plausible is correct. Sometimes plausible is invented.
- **Lesson 3.2:** The categories of tasks where AI is reliable, semi-reliable, and unreliable. Reliable: rewriting, summarizing text you provided, brainstorming, formatting. Semi-reliable: explanations of well-known topics, math, code. Unreliable: specific facts, recent events, citations, anything where being wrong matters and you can't verify.
- **Lesson 3.3:** Verification habits. The two-question check: "Could this be wrong?" and "What happens if it is?" If the second answer is bad, verify before using.
- **Exercise:** Ask the AI a series of questions where you can check the answer. Some it will get right. Some it will get wrong. Some it will get confidently and beautifully wrong. The goal is to feel that gap between confidence and correctness in your own gut.
- **Win:** Student has a personal heuristic for when to trust an AI answer and when to double-check.

## Week 4: Beyond chat, the second tool

Now that the student has one tool down, we add a second category. The point isn't to teach every tool. It's to teach the skill of approaching a new tool, which generalizes.

- **Lesson 4.1:** The map of AI tools today. Categories, not specific products: chat (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini), image (Midjourney, DALL-E), audio/video (ElevenLabs, Suno, Descript), search/research (Perplexity), and AI built into tools the student already uses (Gmail, Google Docs, Notion, Canva).
- **Lesson 4.2:** How to evaluate a new AI tool in 15 minutes. What is it for? What is it bad at? Is the free tier enough for what I want? The framework matters more than the tool.
- **Lesson 4.3:** Picking a second tool that fits the student's life. We give a guided choice: image generation if they create visual content, AI search if they research a lot, audio tools if they're in podcasting or accessibility, etc.
- **Exercise:** Pick a tool from a different category. Use it for a real task. Document the same way: prompts, iterations, lessons learned.
- **Win:** Student has working competence in two categories of AI tools, not just one. The second one was easier than the first, and that fact alone is the most empowering thing in the course.

## Week 5: AI as a thinking partner, not a vending machine

This is where students go from "I use AI for tasks" to "I think differently because of AI." It's the most underrated module in beginner AI education and the one that creates the biggest transformation.

- **Lesson 5.1:** Using AI to think, not just to produce. Asking it to argue against your idea. Asking it to find the holes in your plan. Asking it to play a difficult conversation with you so you can practice.
- **Lesson 5.2:** The personal use cases that matter most: drafting, summarizing, learning new topics, decision support, social/relational tasks (writing a hard message, planning a difficult conversation), creative starting points.
- **Lesson 5.3:** Where to be careful. AI is not a therapist, a doctor, a financial advisor, or a friend. The boundaries are about more than disclaimers; they are about the user's own emotional health.
- **Exercise:** Use AI to think through a real decision the student is facing. Not to make the decision; to surface considerations they hadn't thought of. Compare what the AI surfaced to what they would have come up with alone.
- **Win:** Student has experienced AI as a tool for their own thinking, not just an output machine.

## Week 6: Going forward on your own

The course ends with the meta-skill: how to keep learning when this course is over. This is what makes the difference between a course that creates dependent customers and one that creates capable graduates.

- **Lesson 6.1:** The personal AI workflow. How to integrate AI into things the student already does, without it becoming a side project. Where in their existing day does it belong?
- **Lesson 6.2:** How to keep up without burning out. Three sources to follow (curated by the instructor, kept short). How to ignore 95% of AI news without falling behind. The signal-to-noise problem is real and most people drown in it.
- **Lesson 6.3:** When to learn more, and what to learn next. A guided fork: if you want to go deeper on one tool, here. If you want to learn about AI strategy for your work, here. If you want to start building things, here. We send them somewhere; we don't try to keep them.
- **Final exercise:** Build a personal AI playbook. One page. Their tools, their saved prompts, their guardrails, their next steps. They walk away with a document they own.
- **Final win:** Student can now learn the next AI thing on their own, because they have the underlying mental models. This is the real product.

## What we deliberately left out, and why

Choices about what not to teach matter as much as what to teach. These are the things that show up in most beginner AI curricula but don't earn their place here:

- **AI history.** Interesting, irrelevant to the goal. Cut.
- **How neural networks work mathematically.** The student does not need this to use AI well. Cut. (We make it available as optional reading for the curious.)
- **Coding.** Out of scope. The audience explicitly does not want it.
- **Comprehensive tool tours.** The point is competence, not coverage. Cut.
- **Heavy AI ethics theory.** Practical ethics shows up week 3 (verification) and week 5 (where to be careful). Theoretical ethics in a beginner course tends to either overwhelm or moralize. We integrate ethics in context instead.
- **AI for business strategy.** Different course, different audience. Mentioning where to go next handles this.
